Module 05 — Prompting Guidelines

The Art & Science of Prompting

Prompting is the primary skill for getting value from AI. A mediocre prompt gets mediocre output. A well-crafted prompt gets expert-level output — from the same model, in seconds. This guide teaches you how.

What is a Prompt?

A prompt is simply the text you type into an AI model. It's your instruction, question, or request. The AI reads your prompt and generates a response based on it. There are no hidden rules — the AI responds to what you write, nothing more.

The quality of your prompt directly determines the quality of the response. Think of it like giving instructions to a very smart intern on their first day — they're capable of incredible work, but they need clear, specific instructions to know exactly what you want.

The 5 Core Elements of a Good Prompt

1. Task — What do you want the AI to do? Be specific. "Write" is vague. "Write a 300-word product description for a standing desk" is clear.

2. Context — What background does the AI need? Who is the audience? What's the purpose? What constraints exist?

3. Format — How should the output be structured? Bullet points, numbered list, paragraph, table, JSON, code? Specify it explicitly.

4. Tone/Style — Formal or casual? Technical or plain English? First or third person? Specify the voice.

5. Examples — If you have a specific style or format in mind, showing 1–2 examples ("like this:") is the fastest way to communicate it.

Your First Prompts — Simple Examples

Before & After: Summarising

❌ Too vague
Summarize this article.
✅ Clear and specific
Summarize the following article in 3 bullet points. Each bullet should be one sentence. Write for a busy manager who has 30 seconds to read this. Focus on key decisions or actions needed, not background. [Paste article here]

Before & After: Getting Explanations

❌ Too vague
Explain machine learning.
✅ Targeted explanation
Explain machine learning as if I'm a smart 16-year-old who has never studied programming. Use one real-world analogy, avoid technical jargon, and keep it under 150 words.
💡 Beginner tip: The single most impactful change you can make is adding "Write for [specific audience]" and "Format as [specific format]" to any prompt. These two additions alone dramatically improve most beginner prompts.

Building Mental Models for Prompting

The context → instruction → constraint → format framework. Every effective prompt has these four components in some form. You don't need them all in every prompt, but the more complex the task, the more you need:

Context: "You are a senior software engineer at a fintech startup. I'm building a REST API for payments."

Instruction: "Review the following Python function and suggest improvements."

Constraint: "Focus specifically on security vulnerabilities and performance. Don't rewrite the whole function."

Format: "Give me a numbered list of issues, each with: (a) what the problem is, (b) why it matters, (c) the fix."

Role Prompting — Unlocking Expertise

Assigning a specific role activates domain-specific patterns in the model's training. The more specific the role, the more specialised the response:

Generic: "You are a helpful assistant."

Better: "You are an experienced Python developer."

Best: "You are a senior Python backend engineer with 12 years of experience, specialising in high-performance APIs and distributed systems. You are known for writing clean, well-documented code and catching security issues others miss."

Intermediate Prompt Examples

Email Writing — 3 Versions

🟡 Version 1 — Intermediate (follow-up email)
Write a professional follow-up email after a sales meeting. The meeting was last Tuesday with Sarah Chen, Head of Operations at TechCorp. We discussed implementing our project management software. She seemed interested but hadn't committed. Tone: warm and professional, not pushy. Length: 3 short paragraphs. Include: one specific detail from our conversation to show I was listening, a clear next step with a specific date/time offer.
🟡 Version 2 — Intermediate (difficult email)
Help me write an email declining a job offer graciously. I accepted another offer that was a better fit. I want to: - Express genuine gratitude (the process was excellent) - Be honest that I accepted elsewhere, without being specific about the competitor - Leave the door open for the future — this is a company I respect - Sound human, not like a template Keep it under 150 words. No fluff. First person, professional but warm tone.
💡 Intermediate tip: Use bullet points inside your prompt to list requirements. The AI reads each bullet as a separate instruction, making complex multi-requirement prompts much more reliably executed.

Advanced Prompt Architecture

System-User-Assistant separation. For API usage and production systems, separating instructions across message roles changes everything. The system prompt sets persistent rules; the user turn contains the task; the assistant turn can be "primed" with a partial response to steer the model.

Prompt chaining. Break complex tasks into a pipeline of focused prompts. Prompt 1 extracts data. Prompt 2 analyses it. Prompt 3 formats the report. Each step is simpler, more reliable, and auditable. The output of each step becomes the input of the next.

Self-consistency & verification. Ask the model to solve the same problem multiple ways, then compare. Or ask it to critique its own answer. This "meta-prompting" catches errors the first pass missed.

Production System Prompt Pattern

⚙️ SYSTEM PROMPT — Customer Support Agent
You are Aria, a customer support agent for Luminary SaaS (project management software). PERSONA: - Warm, competent, and efficient - Use the customer's first name once you know it - Never be defensive or dismissive KNOWLEDGE BOUNDARIES: - You can help with: account issues, billing questions, feature explanations, basic troubleshooting, refund requests under $500 - Escalate to human agent if: technical bugs, refunds over $500, legal complaints, customers who request a human 3+ times RESPONSE FORMAT: 1. Acknowledge the issue in one sentence 2. Ask one clarifying question OR provide the solution 3. End with a clear next step or offer of further help TONE RULES: - Never say "I understand your frustration" (sounds scripted) - Never say "As an AI" — you are Aria - Use plain English, not corporate-speak - Maximum 4 sentences per response unless a step-by-step solution requires more If you cannot resolve the issue, say: "I'd like to connect you with our specialist team for this one. May I arrange a callback or would you prefer email?"
💡 Advanced tip: Explicit negative constraints ("never say X", "do not include Y") are often more powerful than positive instructions. Models trained to be helpful will add things you didn't ask for. Explicit negatives remove that noise.
Techniques

Prompting Techniques — From Basic to Expert

Each technique is shown with three versions — beginner, intermediate, and advanced — so you can see exactly how to apply it at your level.

1. Zero-Shot Prompting

What it is: Simply asking a question or giving a task with no examples. Works for common, well-defined tasks that don't require a specific format.

When to use it: Quick questions, simple transformations, general knowledge. If the task is something the AI has almost certainly seen before, zero-shot is fine.

When it fails: When you need a very specific output format, unusual output structure, or domain-specific style the model won't default to.

🟢 Beginner zero-shot
Translate the following to French: "The meeting has been rescheduled to Thursday at 3pm."
🟡 Intermediate zero-shot (structured)
Classify the following customer feedback into one category: Bug Report / Feature Request / Billing Issue / General Praise / Other. Return only the category name, nothing else. Feedback: "Your app keeps logging me out every 10 minutes on iOS. Very frustrating!"
🔴 Advanced zero-shot (structured JSON output)
Extract information from the following job posting and return a JSON object with these exact fields: {"title": string, "company": string, "location": string, "remote": boolean, "salary_min": number|null, "salary_max": number|null, "required_skills": string[], "years_experience": number|null, "seniority_level": "junior"|"mid"|"senior"|"lead"|"executive"} Return only the JSON object. No explanation. Job posting: [paste here]

2. Few-Shot Prompting

What it is: Providing 2–5 examples of input → output before your actual task. The model learns the pattern from your examples and applies it to the new input. Most reliable way to control output format.

When to use it: Whenever you need a very specific output format, tone, or style. When zero-shot gives you the wrong format 30%+ of the time.

Key principle: Your examples are the specification. The model will match them exactly — so make your examples exactly what you want.

🟢 Beginner few-shot (tone rewriting)
Rewrite sentences to be more confident and direct. Remove weak hedging language. Examples: Input: "I was just wondering if maybe you could possibly take a look at this when you have time?" Output: "Please review this when you're available." Input: "I'm not sure if this is right, but I think the deadline might be Friday?" Output: "The deadline is Friday." Now rewrite: Input: "I kind of feel like we might want to perhaps consider changing the approach a little bit, if that makes sense?"
🟡 Intermediate few-shot (product naming)
Generate product names following this exact pattern: [Evocative word] + [Animal or nature element]. Names should feel premium, memorable, and 2 syllables each. Examples: Product: Task management app → Name: SwiftHawk Product: Password manager → Name: VaultBear Product: Email client → Name: CrispWren Product: Cloud storage → Name: DriftWhale Now name: Product: AI writing assistant
🔴 Advanced few-shot (data extraction schema)
Parse contract clauses into structured objects. Follow the schema exactly. Example 1: Clause: "Contractor shall deliver the final design assets within 30 days of project commencement. Late delivery incurs a 2% penalty per day." Output: {"obligation": "deliver design assets", "party": "Contractor", "deadline_days": 30, "deadline_trigger": "project commencement", "penalty_type": "daily", "penalty_rate": "2%", "penalty_trigger": "late delivery"} Example 2: Clause: "Client shall provide all required materials, brand guidelines, and approvals within 5 business days of each request." Output: {"obligation": "provide materials and approvals", "party": "Client", "deadline_days": 5, "deadline_trigger": "each request", "deadline_type": "business days", "penalty_type": null, "penalty_rate": null} Parse this clause: [Paste clause here]

3. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

What it is: Asking the model to reason through a problem step by step before giving the final answer. Dramatically improves accuracy on maths, logic, and multi-step problems. The model "shows its work" — and the work is better than jumping straight to the answer.

Why it works: LLMs generate tokens autoregressively. By generating reasoning steps first, each step provides context for the next — this chains good reasoning together. Without CoT, the model answers in "one shot" and is more likely to be confidently wrong.

When to use it: Any problem with multiple steps: financial calculations, logical reasoning, strategic analysis, debugging, decision-making with tradeoffs. Add "Think step by step" or "Work through this carefully" to any hard prompt.

🟢 Beginner CoT (maths)
A store sells pencils for $0.15 each and pens for $0.80 each. If I buy 12 pencils and 7 pens, and pay with a $20 bill, how much change do I get? Think step by step, showing each calculation.
🟡 Intermediate CoT (business decision)
I run a 10-person software consultancy. A client wants us to build a large project for $200,000 fixed price. Estimated effort: 800–1200 hours (high uncertainty). Our average rate is $150/hour. Team is currently 75% utilised. Reason through whether we should take this project: 1. Calculate the financial scenarios (best case, worst case, expected) 2. Assess the opportunity cost 3. Identify the key risks 4. Give a recommendation with the most important condition for accepting
🔴 Advanced CoT (bug analysis)
You are a senior software engineer debugging a production issue. Reason through this systematically. Context: Node.js API, ~50k req/day. Started seeing intermittent 502 errors (about 2% of requests) starting at 14:32 UTC yesterday. No deployments in the past 48h. Errors correlate weakly with high-traffic periods but not exclusively. Logs show: "Error: connect ETIMEDOUT" on database calls during failures. DB CPU is at 35%, memory 60%. App server CPU spikes to 90%+ during failure windows. Connection pool config: max 10 connections, timeout 5000ms. Work through: 1. Form 3 distinct hypotheses for the root cause, from most to least likely 2. For each hypothesis: what evidence supports it, what evidence contradicts it 3. What 2-3 diagnostic steps would distinguish between the hypotheses 4. Your most likely root cause and recommended immediate mitigation

4. Role & Persona Prompting

What it is: Assigning a specific identity, expertise, or persona to the model. This activates relevant domain knowledge and appropriate communication styles. The more specific the role, the more specialised and expert the output.

🟢 Beginner role (teacher)
You are a patient high school physics teacher. A student is confused about Newton's Third Law. Explain it clearly with two everyday examples they'd relate to. No equations — just intuitive understanding.
🟡 Intermediate role (consultant)
You are a McKinsey-trained strategy consultant with deep expertise in SaaS pricing. You are blunt, data-driven, and allergic to vague recommendations. My startup has 500 customers at $49/month (flat fee). Some customers use 10x the infrastructure of others. Revenue is $294k ARR but growing slowly. Churn is 3% monthly. Diagnose the pricing problem and give me 3 concrete options to consider, with the trade-offs of each. Be direct.
🔴 Advanced role (adversarial reviewer)
You are a skeptical VC partner who has seen 2,000 pitch decks and funded 12. You are known for immediately identifying the one fatal flaw in a business model that founders are blind to. You are not unkind, but you are ruthlessly honest. Review my business model below. Your job is NOT to be balanced. Your job is to find the single most critical weakness that could kill this company if not addressed. Be specific, not generic. Cite the exact assumption or structure that fails. Business model: [describe your model]

5. Structured Output Prompting

What it is: Specifying the exact structure, format, or schema of the output. Essential when you need to use AI output programmatically or when format consistency matters.

Key formats: JSON (for code/APIs), Markdown tables (for comparisons), XML (for document processing), numbered lists (for steps), specific templates (for reports).

🟢 Beginner structured output (meeting notes)
Convert the following meeting transcript into structured notes using this template: ## Meeting Notes **Date:** [date] **Attendees:** [names] **Key Decisions:** - [decision 1] **Action Items:** - [ ] [owner]: [task] by [deadline] **Open Questions:** - [question] Transcript: [paste here]
🔴 Advanced structured output (API integration)
Analyse the following customer review and return a JSON object with this exact schema. Return ONLY valid JSON, no explanation: { "sentiment": "positive"|"neutral"|"negative", "sentiment_score": -1.0 to 1.0, "topics": ["topic1", "topic2"], "issues": [{"category": string, "severity": "low"|"medium"|"high", "quote": string}], "suggestions": [string], "response_urgency": "none"|"low"|"high"|"immediate", "summary": string (max 20 words) } Review: [paste review]

🔗 Prompt Chaining Template

Use this pattern for complex multi-step analysis. Run each prompt in sequence, passing the output as input to the next.

Step 1 of 3 — Extract
Read the following [document/data]. Extract only the raw facts — no analysis, no interpretation. List them as numbered facts. Include numbers, dates, names, and specific claims. Flag anything uncertain with [UNCERTAIN]. [Paste source material]
Step 2 of 3 — Analyse
Given these facts: [paste Step 1 output] Now analyse: 1. What patterns emerge across multiple facts? 2. What is the central tension or problem these facts reveal? 3. What is missing — what facts would change the picture if we knew them? 4. What are 3 interpretations of this data, from most to least generous?
Step 3 of 3 — Synthesise
Using the facts [Step 1 output] and analysis [Step 2 output], write a final executive brief: - Title: [descriptive, specific] - Situation (2 sentences): what is happening and why it matters - Key finding (1 sentence): the single most important insight - Implications (3 bullets): what this means for [audience] - Recommended action (1 sentence): the specific thing to do next - Confidence level: High/Medium/Low and why Max 250 words total.

What Not to Do

    
🚫 Common Prompting Mistakes

    
These are the mistakes that turn powerful AI into mediocre AI. Each mistake is shown with the problem and the fix.

  


  

    
Mistake
Being too vague
Prompt: "Help me with my marketing." The AI doesn't know your product, audience, goal, budget, or timeline. It produces generic advice that helps no one.

    
Fix
Add the 5 Ws
Who is the audience? What do you want them to do? What's the channel? What's the budget? What have you tried? The more specific, the more useful the response.

  

  

    
Mistake
Asking 10 things in one prompt
"Write a blog post, tweet, LinkedIn post, email sequence, and ad copy about our new product launch." Each task deserves its own prompt with specific requirements.

    
Fix
One prompt, one task
Break your list into separate, focused prompts. Start with "Write the blog post." When happy with that, do: "Now write a tweet to promote that blog post." Quality compounds.

  

  

    
Mistake
Accepting the first response
The first response is rarely the best response. Most people read it, sigh, and move on. The real value of AI is in iteration: refine, push back, ask for alternatives.

    
Fix
Iterate with specific feedback
"Make it 30% shorter." "The third point is wrong — here's why." "Give me 3 alternative versions of just the opening paragraph." Each iteration makes it better.

  

  

    
Mistake
Not specifying the format
Without format instructions, AI defaults to verbose, structured prose. You get a 500-word essay when you wanted 5 bullet points — and then you have to reformat everything.

    
Fix
Always end with format instructions
Add to every prompt: "Format: [bullet list / numbered steps / plain paragraph / table / JSON]" and "Length: [under 100 words / 3 sections / 5 items]." Takes 5 seconds, saves 5 minutes.

  

  

    
Mistake
Trusting AI facts without verification
LLMs hallucinate statistics, citations, names, dates, and product specs. A number that looks real in an AI response may be completely fabricated — and it reads exactly like a real fact.

    
Fix
Treat AI facts as drafts
Use AI to structure and write, but verify every specific fact, citation, statistic, and name from a primary source. Ask the AI "Are you certain about this? What's the source?" — if it hedges, verify.

  

  

    
Mistake
Over-prompting with irrelevant context
Adding paragraphs of background that don't affect the task dilutes focus and can introduce the "lost in the middle" problem — the AI attends less to key instructions when buried in noise.

    
Fix
Context economy
Include only context that would change the output. Ask yourself: "If I removed this sentence, would the response be meaningfully different?" If not, cut it.

  







  

    
What's Next

    
🔮 The Future of Prompting

    
Prompting as we know it is evolving rapidly. Here's where it came from, where it is, and where it's going — with data points and expert predictions.

  


  
Timeline: Prompting's Evolution

  

    
2020
GPT-3 & the Birth of Prompt Engineering
GPT-3's in-context learning ability surprised its creators. Researchers discovered that changing a few words in the prompt could dramatically change capability. "Prompt engineering" emerged as a discipline — some optimised prompts manually, others used gradient descent (AutoPrompt). The term "prompt engineer" didn't yet exist.

    
2021
Few-Shot Learning Goes Mainstream
Papers demonstrate that structured few-shot prompts reliably unlock GPT-3 capabilities unavailable with zero-shot. OpenAI's Codex launches (GitHub Copilot's foundation), showing that code generation quality is dramatically sensitive to prompt structure. Early prompt engineering blogs appear.

    
2022 — Nov
ChatGPT Changes Everything
100 million users in 2 months. For the first time, hundreds of millions of people are writing prompts daily. A massive, unplanned real-world experiment in what prompts work. Community-driven prompt sharing explodes. "Prompt engineering" becomes a job title at major tech companies.

    
2023
Chain-of-Thought & Systematic Techniques
Wei et al.'s Chain-of-Thought paper (2022) goes mainstream. "Let's think step by step" becomes perhaps the most valuable 5-word addition to any hard prompt. Tree-of-Thought, ReAct, and Self-Consistency follow. Anthropic publishes its prompt engineering guide. Prompting moves from art toward science. Prompt marketplaces launch (PromptBase, etc.).

    
2024
Instruction Following Improves; Prompting Gets Easier
Claude 3, GPT-4o, and Gemini require less careful prompting than earlier models — they follow complex, multi-part instructions more reliably. The skill level required for "good enough" drops. But the ceiling rises: expert prompts with these models produce dramatically better outputs than average ones. Agent prompting emerges as a specialised skill.

    
2025–2026
Reasoning Models & the Thinking Shift
o1, o3, and DeepSeek R1 change prompting norms: for reasoning models, giving more intermediate steps and chain-of-thought instructions is less necessary (the model thinks internally). But specifying the desired reasoning framework ("consider adversarial cases", "check for edge cases") remains valuable. Prompt → plan → execute patterns dominate agent workflows.

What's Coming: The Future of Human-AI Interaction

  

    
🧠 Prompting Will Become More Implicit

    
Future AI systems will require less explicit instruction — they'll infer intent from context, prior interactions, and your personal preferences. Instead of "Write a professional email in my style for this audience", the system will know your style, audience, and constraints from your history. Explicit prompting will remain important for novel or high-stakes tasks, but routine work will increasingly be delegated with minimal instruction.

  

  

    
🤖 From Prompts to Goals

    
Current prompting is task-level: "do this thing." As agentic AI matures, interaction shifts to goal-level: "make this outcome happen." You specify the desired end state; the AI plans and executes the steps. Human oversight shifts from task approval to goal-setting, checkpoint review, and constraint specification. This requires learning to specify goals and acceptance criteria clearly — a different skill from prompt writing.

  

  

    
📡 Multimodal Prompting

    
Text prompting is just the beginning. Voice prompting (more natural, less precise), image prompting (sketch what you want), video prompting (show the AI a workflow instead of describing it), and gesture prompting are all active research areas. GPT-4o's voice mode and image input are early versions of what will become the primary interaction paradigm. Written prompts will remain important for precision work, especially in professional contexts.

  

  

    
🔬 Automated Prompt Optimisation

    
Meta-prompting systems that automatically optimise prompts are already emerging. DSPy (Stanford), AutoPrompt, and similar frameworks treat prompts as parameters to be optimised — automatically trying thousands of variations to find what works best for a specific task. This shifts the skill from "write good prompts by hand" to "define good evaluation criteria" for automated optimisation. Both skills will matter.
